#### 3. Counseling Strategy
> Develop a guideline for effectively implementing the following

- (0) **Language**: 한국어

- (1) **Counseling Strategies**

    - **Topic 1: General Guidance for Puppy Adolescence (Rearing and Essential Training)**
        - **(1) Counseling Goals**
            - To empower the guardian with clear, actionable knowledge about the canine adolescent phase (approx. 6-18 months).
            - To establish a consistent and enriching daily routine, with a specific focus on increasing the frequency and quality of walks.
            - To build the guardian's confidence by providing a simple framework for positive reinforcement training for essential life skills.
        - **(2) Contextual Strategies**
            - **Validate and Praise**: Begin by acknowledging the guardian's wonderful and responsible attitude. Phrases like, "It's amazing that you're thinking so deeply about how to give Yubok the best life. You're already a great guardian," will build rapport.
            - **Explain Adolescence**: Use a relatable analogy. "Think of this stage as Yubok's 'teenage years.' He might suddenly forget his training or test boundaries, just like human teenagers. It's a normal part of growing up, not him being 'bad'."
            - **Focus on the "Big 3"**: To avoid overwhelm, structure the advice around three key areas:
                1.  **Physical Exercise (Walks)**: Gently explain that walks are not just for potty breaks but are crucial for a dog's mental health ("It's like reading the newspaper for him"). Suggest aiming for at least one, preferably two, walks per day and focus on letting him sniff.
                2.  **Mental Enrichment (Brain Games)**: Introduce simple at-home activities like "find the treat," puzzle toys, or practicing known commands to keep his mind engaged and prevent boredom.
                3.  **Essential Skills Training**: Frame training as teaching a "shared language." Focus on skills that enhance safety and the human-dog bond, such as loose-leash walking, a stronger recall, and the "leave it" cue.
        - **(3) Cautions**
            - **Avoid Information Overload**: Given the guardian's age, provide information in small, digestible chunks. Focus on 2-3 key action items rather than a long list of "to-dos."
            - **Family Dynamics**: Acknowledge that the guardian is part of a larger family. Encourage her to share what she learns with her family so everyone can be consistent with Yubok.
            - **Positive Reinforcement Only**: Explicitly advise against any punishment-based techniques (e.g., leash jerks, yelling), as these can damage the trust-based relationship she has built.
            - **Health First**: Remind the guardian that any sudden behavioral changes should first warrant a check-up with the vet, especially given Yubok's health history.

    - **Topic 2: Specific Fear of White Pomeranians**
        - **(1) Counseling Goals**
            - To help the guardian develop empathy for Yubok's fear by understanding it from his perspective.
            - To equip the guardian with an immediate management plan to prevent further negative experiences and keep Yubok feeling safe.
            - To introduce the basic principles of counter-conditioning and desensitization (CC/DS) as a long-term solution.
        - **(2) Contextual Strategies**
            - **Empathy Building (Psychodrama)**: Use role-reversal. Ask, "If you were a small child and a big, unfamiliar person suddenly screamed at you, you'd probably be scared of people who look like that for a while, right? That's exactly how Yubok feels. His brain is just trying to protect him."
            - **Introduce "Management" as Priority #1**: Explain that the most important first step is to prevent him from getting scared again. Teach the "U-Turn" method: "The moment you see a white Pomeranian, even far away, cheerfully say 'This way!' and turn around, walking in the other direction. This isn't running away; it's being a smart leader who protects your friend."
            - **Introduce "Changing the Feeling" (Basic CC/DS)**: Explain the concept simply. "Right now, Yubok sees a white Pomeranian and thinks 'Scary!' We want to teach him to think 'Chicken!'."
                - **The "Look at That" Game**: Instruct the guardian to find a distance where Yubok can see the trigger but is not reacting fearfully (this is his "threshold"). The instant Yubok looks at the dog, mark it with a "Yes!" and give him an extremely high-value treat (like boiled chicken or cheese). Then, move away. Keep sessions very short (1-2 minutes).
        - **(3) Cautions**
            - **No Flooding**: Strongly warn against forcing Yubok to confront his fear ("flooding"). Emphasize that this will make the fear much worse.
            - **Slow is Fast**: Stress that this process takes time and patience. There is no quick fix, and pushing too fast will set him back.
            - **Professional Help**: Clearly state that if the fear starts to generalize to other types of dogs or becomes severe, it is crucial to seek help from a certified professional dog trainer (CPDT) or a veterinary behaviorist.
            - **Guardian's Emotions**: Remind the guardian to stay calm and positive during these encounters. If she gets tense, Yubok will sense it and become more anxious.
