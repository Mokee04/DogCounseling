#### 3. Counseling Strategy

- **(0) Language**: 한국어

- **(1) Counseling Strategies**

    - **Topic 1: Fear-Based Reactivity and Aggression Towards Strangers**
        - **(1) Counseling Goals**
            - **Short-Term**: The guardian will learn and implement a safety and management plan to prevent any future bites and to stop Maron from practicing his reactive behavior. This includes the successful introduction of a basket muzzle.
            - **Mid-Term**: The guardian will be able to identify Maron's subtle, early stress signals (e.g., stiff body, staring, closed mouth) and proactively create distance from triggers *before* he reacts.
            - **Long-Term**: Through a systematic behavior modification plan, Maron's emotional response to strangers will begin to shift from intense fear to neutrality, allowing for calmer walks and outings.
        - **(2) Contextual Strategies**
            - **Empathy and Reframing (Guardian Mind Care)**: Start by validating the guardian's fear and stress. Use CBT to reframe the thought from "I want him to be loved by others" to "My priority is to help Maron feel safe in the world." This shifts the goal from an uncontrollable outcome (other people's feelings) to a controllable process (the dog's well-being).
            - **Conveying the Dog's Perspective (Psychodrama)**: Use a monologue to articulate Maron's fear. For example: "When I see that man standing there, my heart feels like it's going to explode. It reminds me of a scary time. I have to bark and lunge to make the threat go away. It's the only way I know how to keep my mom and me safe. When he sits down, it's less scary, but when he stands up again, the fear comes right back."
            - **Solution-Focused Plan**:
                1.  **Safety First (Management)**: Immediately implement strict safety measures. This is non-negotiable.
                    - **Muzzle Training**: Introduce a comfortable basket muzzle using positive reinforcement. Frame it as a "safety tool," like a seatbelt, that allows them to work on training without the risk of another bite.
                    - **Environmental Management**: Use window film on the car windows and the shop door/windows to obscure the view of passersby.
                    - **Visitor Protocol**: When visitors come to the home or shop, Maron should be securely in another room or behind a baby gate with a high-value chew (like a frozen Kong). No direct interaction with visitors for now.
                2.  **Behavior Modification (Training)**:
                    - **Distance is Key**: On walks, the guardian's job is to be a "detective" for triggers. The moment she spots one, she should cheerfully say "This way!" and make a U-turn, rewarding Maron with high-value treats as they move away. This teaches him that seeing a trigger means good things (treats) and moving away with his guardian.
                    - **Systematic Desensitization**: Begin formal training sessions at a very large distance from a trigger (e.g., across a park from a stationary person). Mark and reward Maron for simply looking at the person without reacting. The goal is to keep him "under threshold" (calm) at all times.
        - **(3) Cautions**
            - **Prioritize Safety**: Given the Level 3A bite history, the risk of re-offense is high. Emphasize that management is not optional; it is essential for the safety of the public and for Maron's own welfare (preventing him from being put in a situation where he could be deemed dangerous).
            - **MUST RECOMMEND SPECIALIST HELP**: This case requires professional, in-person guidance. Strongly advise the guardian to seek help from a certified veterinary behaviorist or a certified dog behavior consultant (KPA, CPDT-KA, etc.) who uses force-free methods.
            - **NO AVERSIVES**: Explicitly warn the guardian against using any form of punishment (leash jerks, shock collars, yelling, pinning). These methods will worsen fear and aggression and destroy trust.
            - **Medical Check**: Recommend a thorough veterinary check-up to ensure his skin allergies are well-managed, as chronic pain or itching can significantly contribute to behavioral problems.

    - **Topic 2: Separation-Related Distress and Hyper-Attachment**
        - **(1) Counseling Goals**
            - The guardian will establish a clear routine for departures and arrivals that reduces anxiety for both herself and Maron.
            - Maron will learn to engage in calm, independent activities (e.g., chewing on a specific toy) while the guardian is present but not interacting with him.
            - Gradually increase the duration Maron can be left alone calmly, starting with just a few seconds.
        - **(2) Contextual Strategies**
            - **Information Gathering**: First, clarify the "0 hours alone" statement. Understanding the exact daily routine is critical to creating an effective plan.
            - **Solution-Focused Plan**:
                1.  **Decouple Departure Cues**: The guardian should practice picking up her keys, putting on her jacket, and touching the doorknob at random times throughout the day *without leaving*. This breaks the connection between these cues and the stress of departure.
                2.  **Create a "Safe Zone" and Departure Ritual**: Designate a comfortable bed or open crate as Maron's spot. When the guardian needs to leave, she should calmly give him a special, high-value "only when I'm gone" puzzle toy or chew. She should then leave without any emotional fuss. Arrivals should also be calm.
                3.  **Fostering Independence**: Encourage "place" or "mat work" training. Reward Maron for staying on his bed for progressively longer periods while the guardian moves about the house. This teaches him that being physically separate from her is rewarding.
        - **(3) Cautions**
            - **Go Slow**: Progress must be made at the dog's pace. Pushing him to endure being alone for too long will set back the training. The goal is to have many successful, short separations, not one long, failed one.
            - **Potential for Medication**: If the anxiety is severe and prevents any progress with behavior modification, gently suggest that a conversation with a veterinarian or veterinary behaviorist about anti-anxiety medication could be a helpful tool to make training more effective.
            - **Avoid Over-Crutching**: While the goal is not to cause distress, it's important to explain that learning to be alone is a crucial life skill for a dog, just like it is for a child. The goal is to build his confidence, not to avoid the situation entirely.
