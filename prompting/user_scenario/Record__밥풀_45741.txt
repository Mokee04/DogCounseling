#### 3. Counseling Strategy

- **(0) Language**: 한국어

- **(1) Counseling Strategies**

    - **Topic 1: Separation Anxiety**
        - **(1) Counseling Goals**:
            - The guardian will understand that building independence is an act of love that makes the dog feel safer.
            - To establish a predictable routine that includes short, positive periods of separation.
            - Bap-pul will learn to settle on her own (e.g., on a mat) while the guardian is present but not interacting, as a first step toward being alone.
        - **(2) Contextual Strategies**:
            - **Reframe the Narrative (CBT)**: Gently challenge the idea that being with Bap-pul 24/7 is purely beneficial. Reframe "leaving her alone" as "teaching her to be brave and self-confident."
            - **Introduce Independence Training**: Provide a step-by-step guide for "mat training" or "place training." This creates a "safe base" for the dog. Start by rewarding her for staying on the mat for seconds at a time while the guardian is right there, then gradually increase duration and distance.
            - **Desensitize Departure Cues**: Guide the guardian to practice picking up keys, putting on a coat, etc., without actually leaving, and pairing these actions with calm praise or a treat, to break the association with distress.
            - **Use Psychodrama**: Ask the guardian, "Imagine Bap-pul could talk. When she sees you pick up your keys, she might be thinking, 'Oh no, my safe person is leaving and the world is scary without her!' How can we change her thought to, 'Oh, the keys! That means I get my special puzzle toy and a nice nap'?"
        - **(3) Cautions**:
            - **Safety First**: Strongly advise against just leaving her to "cry it out," as this will intensify her panic and can worsen the phobia.
            - **Manage Expectations**: Explain that progress will be very gradual, measured in seconds and minutes at first.
            - **Veterinary Consult**: Recommend a discussion with a veterinarian about anti-anxiety medication. For severe cases, medication can lower the dog's baseline anxiety enough for behavioral modification to be effective.

    - **Topic 2: Sound Sensitivity & General Reactivity**
        - **(1) Counseling Goals**:
            - The guardian will learn to manage the environment to reduce Bap-pul's triggers.
            - Bap-pul's emotional response to common sounds (e.g., doorbell) will shift from fear to neutral or positive anticipation.
            - To increase Bap-pul's daily mental and physical exercise to lower her overall arousal level.
        - **(2) Contextual Strategies**:
            - **Educate on "Trigger Stacking"**: Explain how multiple small stressors (a noise, a person walking by) can add up and lead to a large reaction.
            - **Implement DSCC (Desensitization & Counter-Conditioning)**: Provide a plan to play recordings of trigger sounds (doorbell, etc.) at a very low volume, paired with very high-value treats (like chicken). The sound should be quiet enough that she notices it but doesn't react.
            - **Enrichment Plan**: Brainstorm a daily enrichment schedule beyond the walk, including puzzle feeders for meals, 5-10 minute training sessions, and simple nose work games (hiding treats).
        - **(3) Cautions**:
            - **Do Not Punish**: Emphasize that punishing growling or barking will only increase fear and may lead to the dog suppressing these warnings and biting without notice.
            - **Management is Key**: While training, advise using management tools like playing white noise or music to mask outside sounds, and putting a note on the door asking people to text instead of ringing the bell.

    - **Topic 3: Resource Guarding**
        - **(1) Counseling Goals**:
            - The guardian and other family members will learn and consistently apply safe management protocols to prevent guarding incidents.
            - To teach Bap-pul that an approaching human predicts something even better, not the loss of her item.
            - The guardian will feel confident in managing the situation without confrontation.
        - **(2) Contextual Strategies**:
            - **Safety Protocol First**: The first and most important step is management. Advise the guardian to stop trying to take things from the dog. For safety, items that are highly guarded should not be given freely.
            - **Teach the "Trade-Up Game"**: Explain how to teach Bap-pul to happily trade items. Start with low-value items. Approach, toss a high-value treat nearby, and when she leaves the item to get the treat, pick up the item, then give it right back. This changes the emotional response.
            - **Use Role-Reversal (Psychodrama)**: "Imagine your most prized possession is in front of you. If I walked up looking like I was going to snatch it, you'd feel defensive. But what if every time I walked up, I gave you a gold coin? Soon, you'd be happy to see me approach." This illustrates the principle of counter-conditioning.
        - **(3) Cautions**:
            - **HIGH-RISK BEHAVIOR**: This is the most critical issue for safety. The Level 2 bite indicates potential for escalation. **Strongly recommend consultation with a certified, in-person professional behavior consultant or veterinary behaviorist if the guarding is severe or escalates.**
            - **Consistency is Crucial**: All three adults in the home MUST be on the same page with the "no confrontation" and "trade-up" rules.

    - **Topic 4: Car Anxiety**
        - **(1) Counseling Goals**:
            - To rule out or address any physical causes (motion sickness).
            - Bap-pul will voluntarily and happily enter a stationary car.
            - To gradually build Bap-pul's tolerance for short car journeys.
        - **(2) Contextual Strategies**:
            - **Vet Check First**: The first step is to advise a vet visit to discuss potential motion sickness and treatment options.
            - **Systematic Desensitization**: Break down the process into micro-steps. 1) Treat for looking at the car. 2) Treat for approaching the car. 3) Treat for being near the open door. 4) Treat for jumping in (engine off) to get a special "car-only" toy/treat, then immediately jumping out. Progress only when the dog is relaxed.
            - **Change the Association**: Make the car the location for wonderful things. Feed a high-value chew in the stationary car with the doors open.
        - **(3) Cautions**:
            - **Go at the Dog's Pace**: Pushing the dog too fast will poison the process and set training back significantly.
            - **Safety in the Car**: Discuss safe travel options like a secured crate or a tested car harness, which can also help some dogs feel more secure.
