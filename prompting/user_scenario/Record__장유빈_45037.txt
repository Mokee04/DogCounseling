#### 3. Counseling Strategy

-   **(0) Language**: 한국어

-   **(1) Counseling Strategies**

    -   **Topic 1: Sudden Excessive Bed Licking**
        -   **(1) Counseling Goals**:
            -   To ensure the guardian understands the critical importance of ruling out medical causes by scheduling a veterinary examination as the immediate first step.
            -   To help the guardian identify potential environmental or social stressors that may have recently emerged.
            -   To provide simple management and enrichment strategies to redirect the behavior, *after* medical causes have been ruled out.
        -   **(2) Contextual Strategies**:
            -   Begin with empathy: "It must be worrying to see Sarang-i suddenly start a new behavior like that. It's very observant of you to notice and seek help."
            -   Prioritize medical advice: State clearly and firmly, "Whenever a dog starts a new, repetitive behavior out of the blue, the very first thing we must do is talk to a veterinarian. It's often their way of telling us something hurts or feels wrong inside. Could you make an appointment to get her checked for things like stomach discomfort or dental issues?"
            -   Use Socratic questioning to investigate triggers: "Let's think back to when this started. Were there any changes at home? A new food? A change in your family's schedule? Any new tensions between the pets?"
            -   Introduce the concept of self-soothing: "Sometimes, just like people might bite their nails when they're nervous, dogs will lick to calm themselves down. We need to figure out *if* she's feeling stressed, and why."
        -   **(3) Cautions**:
            -   **DO NOT** provide behavioral modification plans for the licking until a veterinarian has cleared the dog of any underlying medical conditions. This is an ethical and welfare priority.
            -   Avoid diagnosing a specific illness. Stick to advising a general vet check-up.
            -   Be mindful that the guardian is young. Explain concepts clearly and simply, without overwhelming her with jargon.

    -   **Topic 2: Understanding Sarang-i's Personality and Reducing Biting**
        -   **(1) Counseling Goals**:
            -   To reframe Sarang-i's "personality" and "biting" from a problem to a form of communication rooted in early life experiences and fear.
            -   To educate the guardian on the profound impact of the 2-week separation from the mother, fostering deep empathy.
            -   To establish a strict "no-touch" policy on sensitive areas to immediately ensure the guardian's safety and begin rebuilding the dog's trust.
            -   To introduce the concept of cooperative care and management as a long-term, positive solution.
        -   **(2) Contextual Strategies**:
            -   Use reframing and psychodrama: "Let's try to see the world from Sarang-i's perspective. She was taken from her mom before she could learn 'how to be a dog.' She never learned how to say 'please stop' gently. So when she bites, she's not being 'bad,' she's screaming, 'I'm scared! Please respect my space!' It's the only language she knows for that feeling."
            -   Introduce the "Trust Account" metaphor: "Every time we force her to endure a touch she fears, we make a withdrawal from our trust account. Right now, the account is low. Our goal is to make many small, positive deposits by respecting her boundaries and not touching her face or paws at all for now."
            -   Provide concrete, safe management solutions: "For now, the most important rule is safety. We will stop trying to groom her face or touch her paws. When you need to, we can talk about using tools like a basket muzzle with a professional's help. For now, let's focus on avoiding the situations that make her feel she needs to bite."
            -   Use a fictional peer case: "I once knew another Maltese who was also very sensitive about being touched. Her family learned to teach her to place her chin in their hand for treats, and over many months, she learned that hands near her face could be a good thing. It's a long process built on trust."
        -   **(3) Cautions**:
            -   **The Level 3B bite history is serious.** Emphasize that punishment (scolding, hitting) will make the fear and aggression much worse and more dangerous.
            -   **Strongly recommend consultation with a certified, force-free behavior consultant or trainer** for in-person guidance, especially given the bite risk and the guardian's age. This is not something that can be fully solved via chat.
            -   Ensure the advice empowers the young guardian but also protects her. The focus must be on management and prevention, not "fixing" the biting through direct confrontation.
