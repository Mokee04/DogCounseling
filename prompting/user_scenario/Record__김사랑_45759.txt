#### 2. Counseling Topics : Guardian’s Concerns and Root Causes
> Make the following content the central topic of the conversation.

-   **Topic 1: Overexcitement & Impulsivity**
    -   **Situation description**: Jelly shows excessive physical reactions when excited, reacts instantly to any stimulus, and quickly escalates his demands (e.g., whining, barking) if his requests are not met.
    -   **Guardian's Needs**: To understand the cause of this behavior (knowledge acquisition) and learn how to manage it for a calmer household (improving problem behaviors).
    -   **Possible causes**:
        -   **Cause 1: Chronic Under-stimulation**: The current walking schedule of twice a week is severely inadequate for a young puppy. This creates a significant build-up of pent-up physical and mental energy, which manifests as hyperactivity, low impulse control, and a constant search for stimulation.
        -   **Cause 2: Normal Puppy Behavior (Unmanaged)**: Puppies are naturally impulsive and have not yet learned frustration tolerance. Without consistent training in impulse control (e.g., 'wait', 'stay'), this behavior is inadvertently allowed or even reinforced.
        -   **Cause 3: Inadvertent Reinforcement**: The guardian may be unintentionally rewarding the escalation. For example, if Jelly whines and then barks, and the guardian gives in to quiet him, he learns that escalating his behavior is an effective strategy to get what he wants.

-   **Topic 2: Coprophagia (Eating Feces) When Alone**
    -   **Situation description**: Jelly eats his own feces specifically when the guardian is preparing to leave or after she has left him alone.
    -   **Guardian's Needs**: To understand this distressing behavior (knowledge acquisition), find a way to stop it (improving problem behaviors), and likely receive emotional support and reassurance (guardian’s mental care).
    -   **Possible causes**:
        -   **Cause 1: Separation-Related Anxiety**: This is the most probable cause given the context. Coprophagia can be a self-soothing or displacement behavior for anxious dogs. The pet shop origin, early separation from the mother, and long hours alone are all major risk factors for separation anxiety. The act occurs as a direct response to the stress of being left alone.
        -   **Cause 2: Extreme Boredom**: Seven hours is a long time for a puppy to be alone with nothing to do. In the absence of appropriate enrichment, a dog may engage in any available activity, including exploring and consuming feces.
        -   **Cause 3: Medical or Nutritional Issues**: While the context points to a behavioral cause, it is crucial to rule out underlying medical conditions, such as nutrient malabsorption, parasites, or other gastrointestinal issues that can trigger coprophagia.

#### 3. Counseling Strategy

-   **(0) Language**: 한국어

-   **(1) Counseling Strategies**

    -   **Topic 1: Overexcitement & Impulsivity**
        -   **(1) Counseling Goals**
            -   The guardian will understand the direct link between Jelly's lack of daily exercise/enrichment and his overexcitable behavior.
            -   The guardian will commit to creating and implementing a new daily routine that includes more frequent walks and mental stimulation activities.
            -   The guardian will learn at least one basic impulse control game to practice daily with Jelly.
        -   **(2) Contextual Strategies**
            -   **Empathize and Reframe**: Begin by acknowledging how challenging puppy energy can be. Reframe the behavior from a "problem" to a "need." For example: "Jelly's excitement isn't a sign of him being naughty; it's a sign of a very bright, energetic dog telling us he needs more to do. It's like a gifted child being bored in class."
            -   **Psychoeducation with Metaphors**: Explain the concept of an "energy cup." "Imagine Jelly has a big cup of energy that needs to be emptied every day through walks, play, and thinking. Right now, we're only taking a few sips out twice a week, so the cup is always overflowing. That overflow is the hyperactivity you're seeing."
            -   **Provide a Structured, Realistic Action Plan**: Instead of just saying "walk him more," propose a concrete starting plan. "For this first week, let's aim for one short, 15-minute 'sniffari' walk each day. The goal isn't distance, but letting him sniff everything he wants. This is like reading the newspaper for him and is incredibly mentally tiring." Also, introduce one simple enrichment tool, like a snuffle mat or a food-dispensing toy.
        -   **(3) Cautions**
            -   Do not overwhelm the guardian. The change from two walks a week to daily walks is significant. Start small and build success.
            -   Advise against strenuous, high-impact exercise due to Jelly's young age and developing joints. Emphasize the importance of sniffing and mental engagement over physical exhaustion.
            -   Clearly explain that impulse control is a skill that takes time to build, like a muscle. There won't be an overnight fix.

    -   **Topic 2: Coprophagia (Eating Feces) When Alone**
        -   **(1) Counseling Goals**
            -   The guardian will accept that coprophagia in this context is a symptom of emotional distress (anxiety), not a deliberate "bad habit."
            -   The guardian will implement immediate management strategies (e.g., cleaning up) and environmental enrichment (e.g., providing a safe chew toy upon departure).
            -   The guardian will understand the importance of consulting a veterinarian to rule out medical causes.
        -   **(2) Contextual Strategies**
            -   **Prioritize Medical Rule-Out**: State clearly and immediately: "Before we address this as a behavior, it is absolutely essential to talk to your vet. We need to be 100% sure there isn't a medical reason for this. Can you make an appointment this week?"
            -   **Normalize and Validate (Caring the Guardian's Mind)**: Acknowledge the guardian's feelings. "I want you to know that this behavior is very upsetting for many guardians, and it's okay to feel distressed by it. But please hear me: this is not Jelly being 'dirty' or 'disgusting.' From his perspective, it's a coping mechanism for a very powerful feeling of fear or anxiety about being alone."
            -   **Use a Psychodrama Monologue**: Give Jelly a voice to build empathy. "When I see you pick up your bag, my stomach feels all tight. The world feels so scary when you're not here. I don't know what to do with this big, scary feeling, so I find something... anything... to focus on. It's not that I like it, it's just... something to do when I'm afraid."
            -   **Provide a Two-Pronged Action Plan**:
                1.  **Management**: "The first step is management. We must prevent him from being able to practice the behavior. This means cleaning up any feces immediately."
                2.  **Counter-Conditioning**: "Let's change his emotional response to your departure. Every time you leave, give him a special, high-value 'goodbye toy' that he ONLY gets when he's alone, like a Kong stuffed with something delicious and frozen. This starts to make 'goodbye' predict something wonderful."
        -   **(3) Cautions**
            -   **CRITICAL**: Under no circumstances should punishment be recommended. Punishing this behavior will severely increase the dog's anxiety and will likely make the problem worse or lead to other anxiety-related behaviors.
            -   Manage the guardian's expectations. Resolving separation anxiety is a slow process that requires consistency and patience.
            -   Strongly advise against leaving the puppy alone for 7 hours straight. If possible, recommend exploring options like a dog walker or doggy daycare, even for part of the day, to alleviate the pressure on the young puppy.
