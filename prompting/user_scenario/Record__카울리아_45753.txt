#### 3. Counseling Strategy

- **(0) Language**: 한국어

- **(1) Counseling Strategies**

    - **Topic 1: Overexcitement and Reactivity during Walks**
        - **(1) Counseling Goals**:
            - Reframe the guardian's perception of Tori's behavior from "aggressive" or "bad" to "fearful" and "overwhelmed."
            - Equip the guardian with 2-3 simple, immediate management techniques (e.g., creating distance, U-turns, treat scatters) to reduce stress for both Tori and herself on walks.
            - Build the guardian's confidence by affirming that her desire to learn and help Tori is the most important first step.
        - **(2) Contextual Strategies**:
            - **Information Gathering**: Begin by asking for a detailed "play-by-play" of a typical walk, from preparation to return. This will help identify specific triggers and moments of rising arousal.
            - **CBT for the Guardian**: The guardian feels she "doesn't know if she's doing it right." Validate her efforts first. Then, challenge the automatic thought "My dog is aggressive with other dogs." Ask (Socratic Questioning): "You mentioned he doesn't play well with other dogs. Can you describe an interaction? Could it be that he's scared and doesn't know what to do, rather than wanting to fight?"
            - **Psychodrama/Reframing**: Use a monologue to convey Tori's perspective. "I'm outside and everything is so loud and fast! When I see another dog, my heart pounds. I don't know if they're friendly or scary, so I bark loudly to say, 'Please don't come any closer! I'm really scared!'" This reframes the barking as a form of communication, not misbehavior.
            - **Caregiving Solutions**: Provide a short-term action plan. Focus on management, not "fixing."
                - **Behavioral Guideline**: "For the next week, our goal is not to meet other dogs, but to have a calm walk. When you see another dog from a distance, immediately turn around and walk the other way while cheerfully saying 'This way!' and rewarding Tori with a high-value treat once you've created space."
                - **Goal**: To prevent Tori from practicing the reactive behavior and to teach him that seeing another dog predicts good things (treats) and a safe retreat with his guardian.
                - **Precautions**: Advise against any form of punishment (leash jerks, scolding) as it will increase his fear and worsen the reactivity.
        - **(3) Cautions**:
            - Strongly advise against using aversive tools like prong collars, choke chains, or e-collars, explaining they will exacerbate fear and anxiety.
            - Manage the guardian's expectations. Explain that reactivity is a complex issue and improvement is gradual. The initial goal is management and stress reduction, not perfect behavior.
            - If the biting behavior on walks is severe or escalating, recommend consulting a certified professional trainer or veterinary behaviorist for in-person sessions.

    - **Topic 2: Frequent Vomiting**
        - **(1) Counseling Goals**:
            - Ensure the guardian understands the critical importance of a veterinary consultation to rule out medical causes for the vomiting.
            - Provide information on potential behavioral factors (e.g., fast eating) that can be addressed *after* a medical all-clear.
            - Empower the guardian with specific questions to ask her veterinarian to facilitate a productive diagnosis.
        - **(2) Contextual Strategies**:
            - **Information Gathering**: Ask clarifying questions (listed in Section 4) about the type of "hard things," the timing of the vomit, and its appearance. This information is for the vet, not for the counselor to diagnose.
            - **Emphasize Medical Priority**: Use direct and clear language. "Vomiting can be a sign of many different health issues, so the most important first step is to talk to your veterinarian. As a behavior counselor, I can't give medical advice, and Tori's health is the number one priority."
            - **Caregiving Solutions (Conditional)**: "After you have spoken with your vet and they have ruled out any medical problems, we can explore ways to slow down his eating. For example..."
                - **Behavioral Guideline**: Introduce the concept of food puzzles or slow-feeder bowls to make mealtime a longer, more engaging activity and prevent gulping.
                - **Goal**: To use feeding as a form of mental enrichment and to modify the behavior of eating too quickly.
                - **Precautions**: Reiterate that this is *only* to be tried after a vet has confirmed there is no underlying medical condition.
        - **(3) Cautions**:
            - **DO NOT DIAGNOSE.** This is the most critical point. Repeatedly state that you are not a veterinarian. Avoid speculating on specific medical causes.
            - Frame the entire discussion around "gathering information for the vet."
            - Do not let the conversation shift to purely behavioral solutions until the guardian has committed to making a veterinary appointment. Document this recommendation clearly.
